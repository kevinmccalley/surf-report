Surfing Supertubos: Conditions, Tides, and What the Data Says
  The break

  Supertubos is a beach break on the southern edge of the Peniche peninsula, about 20 minutes from where I live. On its best days it produces some of the most powerful, hollow waves in Europe. The WSL holds a Championship Tour event here for a reason — when the swell, wind, and tide align, it's world-class.
  What makes it tricky is that it's unforgiving. The wave pitches fast and hard. If you're not comfortable pulling into a barrel or taking a heavy wipeout, there are friendlier breaks a short drive away. On a good day the lineup is crowded and the locals are out. It is best to check the forecast carefully and   wait for the right window — the difference between a session worth making the drive for and a waste of time comes down to a few degrees of wind and a couple of seconds of period.

  ---
  Best conditions

  Here's what you want to see on the forecast before you commit to the drive:

  Swell direction: NW is the money direction. A pure N swell works too. SW swell tends to close out or go mushy — it wraps around the peninsula the wrong way and loses its shape.

  Swell size: 1.5–2.5m face height. Below 1m and there's not enough power for the wave to do what it does. Above 3m it starts closing out in sections and the paddle-out becomes a serious mission.

  Period: 14 seconds or more. This matters more than size. A 1.5m swell at 8 seconds is nothing like a 1.5m swell at 16 seconds. The long-period Atlantic groundswell is what gives Supertubos its punch and shape. Don't chase size — chase period.

  Wind: Easterly is offshore. E or SE in the morning before the sea breeze kicks in is the ideal. Once you see W or SW in the forecast, the surface turns to chop and the session is over before it starts. Morning sessions almost always beat afternoons here.

  Tide: Mid to low is the sweet spot. High tide pushes too much water over the sandbar and takes away the snap. Very low tide can expose the bar and make the inside dangerously shallow — worth knowing before you drop in.

  ---
  What the data says

  Three and a half years of Groundswell data for Peniche shows what anyone who's surfed this coast already suspects: October is the best month, and it's not close.

  Late September is when the first real Atlantic groundswells of autumn start arriving. October delivers consistent NW swell with manageable wind. November adds more swell volume but also more storm-driven onshore conditions — the waves are bigger on average, but the clean windows are shorter and harder to
  predict.

  Summer is a different story. June through August has plenty of swell, but the Portuguese trade winds push onshore most afternoons, and Supertubos needs offshore wind to hold its shape. Early morning sessions in summer can be good — I've had decent August sessions — but the odds are against you if you're
  traveling specifically for this wave.

  December through February brings the biggest swells of the year. Some of those days are incredible. Most of them are too big, too messy, or both.

  If you're planning a trip and Supertubos is the target, check the historical data at groundswell.surf and filter for October. The pattern is consistent year over year.

  ---
  The honest take

  What Groundswell gets right here: swell height and period accuracy is solid for Peniche. The ECMWF model handles the Atlantic swell corridor well. Most of the time, if the app shows 1.8m at 15 seconds, that's what you find in the water.

  What it sometimes misses: the wind. Peniche is a peninsula and it creates its own localized wind pattern. The difference between a glassy morning and a choppy one can be one or two hours earlier than the regional forecast shows. I've shown up for what looked like a perfect 8am window and found the sea
  breeze had come in early. The app gives you the regional picture — the microclimate on the tip of the peninsula is something you learn from being there.

  The other variable no forecast can see is sandbar quality. A good bar turns an average swell into a great session. The only way to know is to look.
